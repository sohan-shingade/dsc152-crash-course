1 Interpret the slope coefficient Appeared 6x

PQ1Q1PQ2Q2PQ3Q3

Simple regression (one predictor):

"Every 1-[unit] increase in [predictor] is associated with an average difference/increase/decrease of [|coefficient|] [units] in [outcome]."

Multiple regression ("holding constant"):

"Every 1-[unit] increase in [predictor] is associated with an average difference/increase/decrease of [|coefficient|] [units] in [outcome], holding [other variables] constant."

NEVER say "causes" for observational data. Always say "is associated with."

Key phrases grader looks for: "associated with", "on average" or "average difference", "holding __ constant"

  • Q2 "For every 1 percentage point increase in adherence rate, the expected time to recovery decreases by about 0.1892 weeks, on average."
  • PQ2 "Every $1 increase in average monthly rent for a 1-bedroom apartment is associated with an average difference in starting salary of $28.50."
2 Interpret the p-value / state conclusion Appeared 5x

PQ1Q1Q2PQ3Q3

What the p-value IS:

"The p-value is the probability of observing a test statistic at least as extreme as the one observed, assuming $H_0$ is true."

Stating the conclusion (reject):

"Since [p-value] $<$ [α], we reject $H_0$. There is statistically significant evidence that [claim in context], [after adjusting for ___]."

Stating the conclusion (fail to reject):

"Since [p-value] $>$ [α], we fail to reject $H_0$. There is not statistically significant evidence of [claim]."

NEVER say "accept H0" or "the probability H0 is true is [p-value]."

  • Q2 "$H_0: \beta_1 = 0$. Since 0.003 < 0.05, we reject $H_0$. There is statistically significant evidence that adherence rate is associated with recovery time, after adjusting for undernourishment."
  • PQ3 "The p-value is 0.0786. We fail to reject $H_0$ and conclude there is not statistically significant evidence of a decline in admissions rate while including an AR(2) term."
3 Interpret a categorical coefficient Appeared 4x

PQ2Q2PQ3Q3

"Compared to [reference category], [this level] is associated with a [|coefficient|]-[unit] higher/lower predicted [outcome], holding [other predictors] constant."

How R picks the reference: alphabetically first level. "no" before "yes", "negative" before "positive", "rural" before "suburban".

  • PQ2 "The reference category is rural (alphabetically first). The coefficient −8,200 means that suburban jobs are associated with an $8,200 lower average starting salary than rural jobs, among companies with the same number of employees."
  • Q2 "The reference category is undernourishment = 'no'. The coefficient 1.5 for undernourishmentyes means that undernourished patients are associated with a 1.5 weeks longer expected recovery time compared to well-nourished patients, holding adherence rate constant."
4 Interpret an interaction coefficient Appeared 4x

PQ2Q2PQ3Q3

As "difference in slopes":

"The interaction coefficient $\hat{\beta}_3$ is the estimated change in the slope of [X] on [Y] when moving from [reference group] to [other group]."

As "additional effect":

"For [group B], each additional unit of [X] is associated with an additional [|β3|] [units] in predicted [Y] compared to [group A / reference]."

As "difference in effect size":

"The [effect of X on Y] is [|β3|] [units] larger/smaller for [group B] compared to [group A / reference]."

Subgroup equation (MEMORIZE THIS PATTERN):

Full model: $\hat{y} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x + \beta_2 G + \beta_3 (x \cdot G)$
When $G = 0$: $\hat{y} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x$
When $G = 1$: $\hat{y} = (\beta_0 + \beta_2) + (\beta_1 + \beta_3) x$

Plug in, combine like terms, simplify.
  • PQ2 "The salary advantage of being in a high-rent city is $9,800 larger for remote jobs than for in-person jobs."
  • Q3 "For coached students, each additional practice hour is associated with an additional 0.10 thousand dollars in predicted stipend compared to uncoached students."
  • PQ3 "For CS applicants, each additional point on the GRE is associated with an average stipend that is $90 higher compared with the corresponding increase for Statistics applicants."
5 "Can we conclude causation?" Appeared 3x

PQ1PQ2Q2

For observational data (answer: NO):

"This statement is incorrect. This is an observational dataset — we did not randomly assign [subjects] to [groups/conditions]. A significant p-value indicates a statistically significant association, not causation. Confounders such as [specific variable] or other variables that we don't even have data on may be driving both [X] and [Y]."

For experiments with random assignment (answer: YES):

"Yes, because subjects were randomly assigned to treatment groups. Random assignment distributes potential confounders equally across groups, so any significant difference can be attributed to [the treatment] itself."

7 Partial F-test (anova) — when and how Appeared 5x

PQ2Q2PQ3Q3

When to use: Testing whether a GROUP of coefficients are all zero simultaneously (categorical variable with 3+ levels, or main effect + interaction).

null_model <- lm(y ~ [predictors WITHOUT the variable], data = df)
full_model <- lm(y ~ [predictors WITH the variable], data = df)
anova(null_model, full_model)

$H_0$: $\beta_{\text{var1}} = \beta_{\text{var2}} = \ldots = 0$ (all coefficients for the tested variable are zero simultaneously)

Interpreting the result:

"Reject $H_0$; there is statistically significant evidence that at least one [group] differs in mean [outcome], adjusting for [other predictors]."

Individual p-values from summary() are NOT the same as the partial F-test when testing multiple coefficients jointly. The individual p-value for coachingyes only tests the coaching effect at practice_hours = 0.

  • Q3 reduced_model <- lm(stipend ~ practice_hours, data = career_df)
    anova(reduced_model, model1)
  • PQ3 "$H_0: \beta_4 = \beta_5 = 0$. The null model is lm(stipend ~ gre + field, data=grad)."
8 Log-transformation interpretations Appeared 3x

PQ3Q3

Log-linear: lm(log(y) ~ x)

"A 1-unit increase in [x] is associated with approximately a [β × 100]% change in [y]."

Double-log: lm(log(y) ~ log(x))

"A 1% increase in [x] is associated with approximately a [β]% change in [y]."

Linear-log: lm(y ~ log(x))

"A 1% increase in [x] is associated with a [β/100]-unit change in [y]."

The model is STILL linear regression even with log(x) — "we simply transformed the predictor before fitting."

  • PQ3 "A 1% increase in GRE score is associated with an approximately 2.84% increase in predicted stipend, holding all else equal."
9 Logistic regression coefficient (log-odds + OR) Appeared 3x

PQ3Q3

Log-odds interpretation:

"Each 1-unit increase in [x] is associated with a [β] increase in the log-odds of [outcome], holding [other vars] constant."

Odds ratio interpretation:

"The odds ratio is $e^{\beta}$ $\approx$ [value]. Each 1-unit increase in [x] multiplies the odds of [outcome] by approximately [e^β], holding [other vars] constant."

CI for odds ratio:

"Exponentiate both endpoints: $(e^{\text{lower}}, e^{\text{upper}})$. We are 95% confident that a 1-unit increase in [x] multiplies the odds of [outcome] by a factor between [e^lower] and [e^upper]."

Multi-unit change: For a $k$-unit increase, log-odds changes by $k\beta$, so odds multiply by $e^{k\beta}$.

  • PQ3 "$e^{0.08} \approx 1.08$. Each additional point on GRE is associated with a multiplicative increase of approximately 1.08 in the estimated odds of admission (roughly 8%), holding GPA constant."
  • Q3 "The odds ratio for coaching is $e^{1.10}$. Holding portfolio score fixed, coached students have $e^{1.10}$ times the odds of receiving an offer compared to uncoached students."
10 Predicted probability (logistic) Appeared 2x

PQ3Q3

Step 1: log-odds $= \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \beta_2 x_2 + \ldots$ (plug in values)
Step 2: odds $= e^{\text{log-odds}}$
Step 3: $\hat{p} = \dfrac{e^{\text{log-odds}}}{1 + e^{\text{log-odds}}}$
  • PQ3 "Log-odds = $-14.82 + 0.08(160) + 0.77(3.5)$. $\hat{p} = \frac{e^{\text{log-odds}}}{1 + e^{\text{log-odds}}}$"
11 Odds ratio from a 2×2 table Appeared 2x

Q3

$RR = \dfrac{\text{risk}_{\text{exposed}}}{\text{risk}_{\text{unexposed}}} = \dfrac{a/(a+b)}{c/(c+d)}$

$OR = \dfrac{\text{odds}_{\text{exposed}}}{\text{odds}_{\text{unexposed}}} = \dfrac{a/b}{c/d} = \dfrac{ad}{bc}$

Interpretation: "The odds of [outcome] are [OR] times as large for [exposed] as for [unexposed]."

OR ≈ RR when: "The outcome is rare (low prevalence)."

Case-control: "RR is invalid (groups are fixed by design). OR is still valid."

  • Q3 "$OR = \frac{72 \cdot 120}{48 \cdot 60} = 3.0$. The odds of receiving an offer are estimated to be 3 times as large for coached students as for uncoached students."
12 "Why is lm() inappropriate for time series?" Appeared 3x

PQ3Q3

"If time is ignored, residuals may still contain a systematic [weekly/temporal] pattern. Errors from nearby [days/time points] may not behave like independent random noise, so ordinary linear-regression standard errors, p-values, and confidence intervals may be misleading."

The fix:

model <- Arima(y, order = c(p, 0, 0), xreg = x)
coeftest(model)

Power simulation trap:

"lm() appears to have higher power (higher rejection rate), but this is actually inflated Type I Error from ignoring autocorrelation, not genuine power. Arima() has the correct Type I Error rate."
  • Q3 lm(log(visits) ~ bookings + day_of_week, data = traffic_df)
  • PQ3 mod <- Arima(admit, order=c(2, 0, 0), xreg=1:50)
    coeftest(mod)
13 Centering & scaling covariates Appeared 2x

PQ3Q3

What centering does:

$x_{\text{centered}} = x - \bar{x}$

"Centering makes the intercept correspond to a [subject] with average predictor values, rather than a [subject] with zero [predictor] (which may not be meaningful)."

What scaling does:

$x_{\text{scaled}} = \dfrac{x - \bar{x}}{2s_x}$

"Scaling makes coefficients interpretable in standard-deviation units: a one-unit increase in the scaled predictor means a [one-standard-deviation / 2-SD] increase in the original predictor."

Why scale by $2s$ (not $s$)? So that a 1-unit change in the scaled continuous predictor is comparable in magnitude to a 0→1 change in a binary predictor.

What does NOT change:

• Centering or scaling does NOT change the p-value for whether a coefficient equals 0.
• Centering and scaling does NOT change $R^2$.
• Centering and scaling is NOT a substitute for checking model conditions (LINE).

Interpreting the intercept (centered model):

"$\hat{\gamma}_0$ is the estimated [outcome] for a [subject] with average [predictor 1] and average [predictor 2]."

Interpreting interaction with centered/scaled covariates:

"$\hat{\gamma}_3$ is the estimated change in the slope on [predictor 1 scaled] for each 1-point increase in [predictor 2 centered] (equivalently, for each 1-point increase in [original predictor 2] above the mean)."

Comparing relative importance:

"With both predictors centered and scaled, $|\hat{\gamma}_1| \gg |\hat{\gamma}_2|$ suggests that [predictor 1] has a substantially larger association with [outcome] than [predictor 2] does, at average levels of the other covariate."
  • PQ3 "When gre = mean(gre) and gpa = mean(gpa), we have gre_s = 0 and gpa_c = 0, so stipend_hat = γ0. Thus γ0 is the estimated stipend for an applicant with average GRE score and average GPA."
  • Q3 "Centering makes the intercept correspond to a student with average predictor values, rather than a student with zero practice hours and zero portfolio score. Scaling also makes coefficients interpretable in standard-deviation units."
  • Q3 "Neither centering a covariate nor scaling it will change the p-value for whether its coefficient is equal to 0."
14 Model selection → inference fallacy (Lab 9) Appeared 2x

Q3 + Lab 9

"Is this workflow valid for inference?"

"This is an incorrect workflow because the p-value for [predictor] from the smaller model is influenced by model-selection based on the same data. The decision of whether to include [other predictor] in the model should not be based on this workflow, but rather on whether there is scientific justification for it as a confounding variable."

"Should you remove this non-significant variable?"

"No. Non-significant p-values do not justify removing pre-specified covariates. Removing them based on the data is model selection, which invalidates subsequent inference. For categorical variables (e.g., day of week): you must keep all levels or remove all — you cannot remove individual levels."

"Is this a valid justification for log-transforming?"

Valid: "Previous studies showed improved model fit on the log scale" (a priori justification).
Invalid: "Looking at our data shows the log scale gives a better fit" (this IS model selection).
Invalid: "Log scale makes coefficients easier to interpret" (convenience ≠ statistical justification).
  • Q3 "This is an incorrect workflow because the p-value for practice_hours from the smaller model is influenced by model-selection step based on the same data."
  • Q3 "The decision of whether to include practice_hours in the model should not be based on this workflow, but rather on whether there is scientific justification for it as a confounding variable."
  • Q3 "As a sensitivity analysis, these residual plots would suggest that future studies should consider using model_logy for statistical inference. Scientific rationale should also guide model choice."

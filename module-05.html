Part A Learn: Statistical vs Practical Significance
01 The core problem: significant ≠ important

Imagine a doctor tells you: "Lab results show your cholesterol is statistically significantly higher than normal." Scary, right?

Then they add: "It's 200.3 instead of 200.0."

Would you change your diet for a 0.3-point difference? Probably not. That's the whole point of this module.

The distinction:
Statistical significance = the p-value is small enough to reject $H_0$
Practical (effect-size) significance = the actual difference is large enough to matter in the real world

These are independent. You can have one without the other:

Practically importantNOT practically important
Statistically significantBest case: real and meaningfulTRAP: huge $n$ detects a useless difference
NOT statistically significantPossible: low power missed a real effectNothing here

The bacon example (Lec 5)

WHO study: eating processed meat increases colon cancer risk by 18%.

Sounds terrifying? The baseline risk is ~5%. An 18% increase means:

Baseline risk: 5.0% After processed meat: 5.0% × 1.18 = 5.9% Absolute increase: 0.9 percentage points

Compare: smoking increases lung cancer risk by 1,500–3,000%. The meat finding is statistically significant (huge study, tiny p-value) but the practical impact is small.

The p-value tells you WHETHER a difference exists. It does NOT tell you whether the difference is large enough to matter.
02 Why large $n$ causes the trap

Recall the t-statistic formula:

$$t_s = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu_0}{s / \sqrt{n}}$$

As $n$ gets huge, $s/\sqrt{n}$ gets tiny. So even a microscopic difference $\bar{x} - \mu_0$ produces a massive $t_s$, which gives a tiny p-value.

THE TRAP IN ACTION

$H_0: \mu = 20$, $n = 50{,}000$, $\bar{x} = 20.3$, $s = 15$

$t_s = \frac{20.3 - 20}{15 / \sqrt{50000}} = \frac{0.3}{0.067} = 4.47$

p-value $= 0.0001$ → Reject $H_0$!

But the actual difference is 0.3 minutes. Nobody cares about 18 seconds.

Rule of thumb: When $n$ is very large, always check the confidence interval to see how big the effect actually is. A tight CI near the null value = statistically significant but practically meaningless.

From the scenario above, 95% CI might be $(20.17, 20.43)$. The entire interval is within half a minute of 20. Operationally useless.

03 CIs tell you more than p-values

The p-value is binary: significant or not. The confidence interval shows you the range of plausible values for the true parameter.

Meaningful CI

$H_0: \mu = 20$
95% CI: $(25.3, 34.7)$

Excludes 20 → reject.
True mean is 5–15 minutes higher. That matters.

Useless CI

$H_0: \mu = 4.00$
95% CI: ($4.01, $4.05)

Excludes 4 → reject.
True mean is 1–5 cents higher. Nobody cares.
Both CIs lead to rejecting $H_0$. But only one represents a difference worth acting on. The CI shows the effect size; the p-value doesn't.
04 The reverse: fail to reject ≠ no effect

Module 4 taught power. Here's where it connects:

SCENARIO FROM QUIZ 1 Q5b

Small pilot: $n = 12$ deliveries from Premium restaurants
Sample mean tip: $\bar{x} = $ $6.80 (vs $H_0: \mu = 4$)
p-value: $0.11$ → Fail to reject at $\alpha = 0.05$

But $6.80 is WAY above $4.00! The sample mean is $2.80 higher. That's a huge practical difference.

So why didn't we reject? Low power. With only $n = 12$, we don't have enough data to detect the effect, even though it's probably real.

"Fail to reject" does NOT mean "no difference exists." It means we don't have enough evidence. With small $n$, the test might just be too weak (low power) to detect a real difference.
The two scenarios side by side:

Large $n$ + tiny effect + small p: "We reject $H_0$, but the difference may not be enough to act on." Check the CI.

Small $n$ + large effect + big p: "We fail to reject $H_0$, but a larger sample size may be needed to determine whether there is a difference." Don't conclude there's no effect.
Part B On the Exam: Significance Questions
05 Exam Q Type: "What concept does this illustrate?"

Real exam question (Practice Quiz 1 Q7b):

The airport collects a large sample of $n = 50{,}000$ passengers and runs a t-test of $H_0: \mu = 20$ vs $H_A: \mu \neq 20$. Sample mean $\bar{x} = 20.3$, $s = 15$. The resulting p-value is 0.0001.

Despite the highly significant p-value, a manager argues that a 0.3-minute difference in average wait time is operationally meaningless for the airport. In 2–3 sentences, explain what concept this scenario illustrates and why the p-value alone can be misleading in large samples.

Loses points

"The p-value is wrong because the difference is small."

The p-value isn't wrong — the test correctly detected a real (but tiny) difference. Missing the key term.

Full marks

"This illustrates the distinction between statistical significance and practical (effect-size) significance. With a very large sample size, even a tiny true difference from the null value will produce a very small p-value, causing us to reject $H_0$. However, a 0.3-minute difference is negligible from an operational standpoint — it provides no meaningful guidance for improving passenger experience. The p-value tells us whether a difference exists, but not whether it is large enough to matter."
Wording keys for full marks:
1. Name the concept: "statistical significance vs practical (effect-size) significance"
2. Explain WHY: large $n$ makes tiny differences detectable
3. Say the p-value tells you "whether" not "how much"
Your Turn

A hospital tests whether a new drug lowers blood pressure. With $n = 100{,}000$ patients, they find $\bar{x} = 119.8$ mmHg vs $H_0: \mu = 120$. p-value = 0.0003. The 95% CI is $(119.7, 119.9)$. Explain what concept this illustrates.

This illustrates the distinction between statistical significance and practical (effect-size) significance. With $n = 100{,}000$, even a 0.2 mmHg difference is detectable, producing a very small p-value. However, a 0.2 mmHg reduction is clinically negligible — the 95% CI of (119.7, 119.9) shows the true mean is at most 0.3 mmHg below 120, which would not change any treatment decisions. The p-value tells us a difference exists, but not whether it is large enough to matter.
06 Exam Q Type: "Select all that are true" — large $n$, tiny effect

Real exam question (Quiz 1 Q5a):

$n = 40{,}000$ deliveries. $H_0: \mu = 4$, $H_A: \mu \neq 4$.
Sample mean tip = $4.03, $s =$ $1.60, p-value $= 0.0002$.
95% CI = ($4.01, $4.05).

At $\alpha = 0.05$, select all that are true:

◼ Since the p-value is extremely small, we reject $H_0$ at $\alpha = 0.05$
◻ Since the sample mean is only $0.03 away from the null hypothesis value, we actually do not reject $H_0$ at $\alpha = 0.05$ even though the p-value is small
◼ Since the 95% confidence interval is very close to the null hypothesis value, the difference from $4 may not be enough to act on even though we should reject $H_0$
◻ Since the p-value is extremely small, this indicates that there is a meaningful difference from $4 even though the estimated mean is not very different from $4

Why each wrong answer is wrong:

  • "We don't reject even though p is small" — WRONG. $p < \alpha$ means we ALWAYS reject. The decision rule is mechanical.
  • "Meaningful difference" — WRONG. Small p-value does NOT mean meaningful. It means detectable. The CI shows it's only 1–5 cents.
TRAP: Students confuse "we should act on this" with "we should reject." They are DIFFERENT things.
Always reject when $p < \alpha$. But then ask: is the effect large enough to care about?
07 Exam Q Type: "Fail to reject but large sample mean" — small $n$

Real exam question (Quiz 1 Q5b):

A small pilot study of $n = 12$ deliveries from Premium restaurants finds a sample mean tip of $6.80 with a p-value of 0.11 for the test $H_0: \mu = 4$ vs $H_A: \mu \neq 4$. Select all correct responses.

◻ We reject $H_0$ at $\alpha = 0.05$ since the sample mean is so different from $4
■ If the true mean tip is $4 and the conditions of the test were met, then the probability of observing a tip that is at least $6.80 or at most $1.20 is 11%
◻ We fail to reject $H_0$ at $\alpha = 0.05$ and should end the investigation here, concluding that there is no meaningful difference from $4
■ The result indicates that a larger sample size may be needed to determine whether there is a difference from $4.

Why each wrong answer is wrong:

  • "Reject because sample mean is far" — WRONG. We compare p-value to $\alpha$, not the sample mean to the null. $0.11 > 0.05$ means fail to reject.
  • "End the investigation" — WRONG. Fail to reject $\neq$ no difference. With $n = 12$, the test has low power. The $2.80 difference might be real but undetectable at this sample size.
Key wording (Quiz 1 Q5b answer key):
"The result indicates that a larger sample size may be needed to determine whether there is a difference from $4."

This is the correct response when you fail to reject with small $n$ but see a large sample mean difference.
Your Turn

A pilot study with $n = 8$ patients tests $H_0: \mu = 120$ (blood pressure). Sample mean = 135, p-value = 0.09. A colleague says "we failed to reject, so the drug doesn't work." What's wrong with this conclusion?

Failing to reject $H_0$ does not mean the drug has no effect. With only $n = 8$, the test has low power — it may simply be too weak to detect the 15-point difference. The sample mean of 135 is substantially above 120, suggesting a real effect might exist. A larger sample size may be needed to determine whether there is a true difference.
Part C Quiz: Statistical vs Practical Significance
08 4-Question Quiz
Q1

In one sentence, explain the difference between statistical significance and practical significance. Then: which one does the p-value measure?

Statistical significance means the p-value is below $\alpha$, indicating sufficient evidence to reject $H_0$. Practical significance means the detected difference is large enough to matter in the real world. The p-value measures statistical significance only — it tells you whether a difference exists, not whether it's large enough to be useful.
Q2

A company tests $H_0: \mu = 50$ with $n = 200{,}000$. They get $\bar{x} = 50.01$, $p = 0.00004$, 95% CI $= (50.005, 50.015)$.

(a) What is the decision at $\alpha = 0.05$?
(b) Should the company act on this finding? Why or why not?

(a) Reject $H_0$ since $p = 0.00004 < 0.05$.
(b) Probably not. The 95% CI is $(50.005, 50.015)$ — the true mean is at most 0.015 units above 50. This is a statistically significant but practically negligible difference. The huge sample size made this tiny effect detectable, but the effect is too small to have any real-world impact.
Q3

A study with $n = 15$ tests $H_0: \mu = 30$ and gets $\bar{x} = 42$, $p = 0.08$. True or false: "Since we fail to reject at $\alpha = 0.05$, we can conclude the true mean is 30." Explain.

False. Failing to reject $H_0$ does not mean $H_0$ is true. With $n = 15$, the test likely has low power, so it may simply be unable to detect the difference. The sample mean of 42 is 12 units above the null value, which suggests a potentially large real effect. A larger sample size may be needed to determine whether there is a true difference from 30.
Q4

Match each scenario to the correct interpretation:

A: $n = 500{,}000$, $\bar{x} = 100.002$, $H_0: \mu = 100$, $p = 0.0001$
B: $n = 10$, $\bar{x} = 115$, $H_0: \mu = 100$, $p = 0.12$

(i) "Larger sample needed to determine if difference exists"
(ii) "Reject, but difference may not be enough to act on"

A → (ii): Reject $H_0$ (tiny p), but $\bar{x}$ is only 0.002 above 100. Statistically significant, not practically significant.
B → (i): Fail to reject (p = 0.12 > 0.05), but $\bar{x} = 115$ is 15 units above null. Low power from $n = 10$. A larger sample may be needed.

Part A Learn: Multiple Regression & "Holding Constant"
01 Why multiple regression — controlling for confounders

In Module 6 you learned that confounders can create fake relationships in observational data. In Module 7 you fit a simple regression with one predictor. But what if that one predictor is tangled up with other variables?

Multiple regression lets you include multiple predictors so you can estimate the effect of one variable while controlling for the others.

Classic example (from lecture): A naive comparison shows smokers have higher lung capacity (FEV) than non-smokers. That seems backwards! But older children are more likely to smoke AND have bigger lungs. Age is a confounder. Once you add age to the model, the smoking effect flips to negative — as expected.

Simple regression (misleading)

lm(fev ~ smoke)
Smoking coefficient: +0.71
Looks like smoking helps lungs?!

Multiple regression (correct)

lm(fev ~ smoke + age)
Smoking coefficient: −0.21
After controlling for age, smoking hurts

This is why multiple regression matters — it helps you untangle confounded relationships.

02 The multiple regression model
$$\hat{y} = \hat{\beta}_0 + \hat{\beta}_1 x_1 + \hat{\beta}_2 x_2 + \ldots$$
SymbolNameWhat it represents
$\hat{\beta}_0$InterceptPredicted $y$ when ALL predictors = 0
$\hat{\beta}_1$Slope for $x_1$Change in $y$ per 1-unit increase in $x_1$, holding all other predictors constant
$\hat{\beta}_2$Slope for $x_2$Change in $y$ per 1-unit increase in $x_2$, holding all other predictors constant
The big difference from simple regression: Each coefficient now has the phrase "holding all other predictors constant" attached to it. This is the single most important idea in this module.

Example (FEV data):

&hat;y = 0.37 + (−0.21) · smoke + 0.23 · age β̂1 = −0.21 → Smoking is associated with a 0.21 L decrease in FEV, holding age constant. β̂2 = 0.23 → Each additional year of age is associated with a 0.23 L increase in FEV, holding smoking status constant.
03 R code: adding predictors with +
multiple_regression.R
# Simple regression (one predictor)
mod_simple <- lm(fev ~ smoke, data = FEV)

# Multiple regression (two predictors)
mod_adj <- lm(fev ~ smoke + age, data = FEV)

# View the full summary
summary(mod_adj)

Adding a predictor is literally just adding + variable_name to the formula. You can add as many as you want:

more_predictors.R
# Three predictors
lm(fev ~ smoke + age + height, data = FEV)

# ds_jobs example from PQ2
mod_adj <- lm(sal ~ emp_n + loc_type, data = ds_jobs)
Common mistake: Writing lm(fev ~ smoke * age) instead of lm(fev ~ smoke + age). The * adds an interaction term, which is a different (more advanced) model. For this class, use + to add predictors.
Reading the output: The summary() table works the same as Module 7 — Estimate, Std. Error, t value, Pr(>|t|) — but now there's a row for each predictor. Each row's p-value tests whether that specific coefficient is zero, holding the other predictors in the model.
04 "Holding constant" interpretation — the key exam phrase

This is the most heavily tested concept from Lecture 8. You WILL see a question asking you to interpret a coefficient from a multiple regression.

"Holding constant" interpretation template:
"For every 1-unit increase in $x_1$, $y$ changes by $\hat{\beta}_1$, on average, holding $x_2$ constant."

Alternative phrasing (equally correct):
"...among [subjects] with the same value of $x_2$."

Loses points

"Smoking decreases FEV by 0.21 liters."

Missing "holding age constant"! Also says "decreases" (causal) instead of "associated with."

Full marks

"Smoking is associated with an average decrease in FEV of 0.21 liters, holding age constant."

Or equivalently: "...when comparing children of the same age."

05 Why coefficients change — Simpson's paradox & confounding

When you add a variable to a regression, the other coefficients often change. Sometimes they even flip sign. This happens because the new variable was confounding the original relationship.

Simpson's Paradox: A trend that appears in the overall data reverses when you split the data into groups (or add a grouping variable to the model). It's the most dramatic version of confounding.

FEV example:

Simple: lm(fev ~ smoke) → smoke = +0.71 Multiple: lm(fev ~ smoke + age) → smoke = −0.21 The sign FLIPPED! Why? • Older kids smoke more (age ↔ smoke) • Older kids have bigger lungs (age ↔ fev) • Age confounds the smoke-fev relationship • Once we control for age, smoking hurts

Penguin example from lecture (also Simpson's Paradox):

Overall: Beak length and beak depth are negatively correlated. But within each species, they're positively correlated. Species is the confounder — different species have very different beak shapes, masking the true within-species relationship.

Bottom line: coefficients change because the model is now separating the effect of each predictor instead of lumping them together.

Part B On the Exam: Multiple Regression Questions (Practice Quiz 2)
06 PQ2 Q2a: What makes a variable a confounder?

Practice Quiz 2, Question 2a:

What conditions must be met for a variable to be a confounder in the relationship between X and Y?

Answer key (exact wording):
A confounder "must be associated with both the primary predictor AND the outcome."
The two-check test:
1. Is the variable associated with X (the predictor)?
2. Is the variable associated with Y (the outcome)?

Both must be YES for it to be a confounder. If it's only related to one, it's not a confounder.

Example: Is loc_type a confounder in the relationship between emp_n and sal?

Check 1: Is loc_type related to emp_n? → Yes: urban areas have bigger companies ✓ Check 2: Is loc_type related to sal? → Yes: urban areas have higher salaries ✓ Both checks pass → loc_type IS a confounder
Your Turn

In a study of the relationship between exercise (exercise_hrs) and weight (weight), is diet_quality a potential confounder? Apply the two-check test.

Check 1: Is diet_quality associated with exercise_hrs? Yes — people who exercise more tend to eat healthier.
Check 2: Is diet_quality associated with weight? Yes — better diet is associated with lower weight.
Both checks pass, so diet_quality is a potential confounder.
07 PQ2 Q2b: Writing simple vs adjusted regression code

Practice Quiz 2, Question 2b:

Write R code for (1) a simple regression predicting sal from emp_n, and (2) an adjusted model that also controls for loc_type.

pq2_q2b.R
# Simple regression (unadjusted)
mod_simple <- lm(sal ~ emp_n, data = ds_jobs)

# Adjusted model (controlling for loc_type)
mod_adj <- lm(sal ~ emp_n + loc_type, data = ds_jobs)
Interpreting the adjusted slope:
If the coefficient for emp_n in mod_adj is 0.85, you say:

"Every 1-unit increase in number of employees is associated with an average difference in starting salary of $0.85, holding location type constant."

Or equivalently: "...among companies with the same number of employees."
Why "adjusted"? The second model "adjusts for" or "controls for" loc_type. This means it compares salaries across companies of different sizes within the same location type, removing the confounding influence of urban vs rural.
08 Interpreting adjusted vs simple slopes — full walkthrough

The exam loves asking you to compare the simple and adjusted models side by side.

Simple: lm(sal ~ emp_n)

emp_n slope: 1.50
Interpretation: Every 1-unit increase in company size is associated with $1.50 higher salary.

Adjusted: lm(sal ~ emp_n + loc_type)

emp_n slope: 0.85
Interpretation: Every 1-unit increase in company size is associated with $0.85 higher salary, holding loc_type constant.
Why did the coefficient shrink from 1.50 to 0.85?
Because part of the original relationship was due to confounding by loc_type. Bigger companies tend to be in urban areas, and urban areas have higher salaries. The simple model was giving emp_n credit for the salary bump that was actually due to location.
Your Turn

A simple regression of GPA on study hours gives a slope of 0.30. After adding motivation_score as a second predictor, the slope for study hours drops to 0.12. Explain why the coefficient changed.

Motivation is a confounder — it is associated with both study hours (more motivated students study more) and GPA (more motivated students earn higher grades). In the simple model, study hours was getting "credit" for the GPA boost that was actually driven by motivation. Once we control for motivation, the remaining effect of study hours shrinks to 0.12.

Q2 — Confounder Definition

A researcher studies the relationship between screen time (screen_hrs) and sleep quality (sleep_score). A classmate suggests that caffeine_intake is a confounder. What two conditions must caffeine_intake satisfy to be a confounder?

To be a confounder, caffeine_intake must be associated with both:
1. The predictor (screen_hrs) — e.g., people who drink more caffeine may have more screen time
2. The outcome (sleep_score) — e.g., caffeine directly disrupts sleep quality
Both conditions must hold.

Q4 — Why Coefficients Change

In a simple regression of test scores on class size, the slope for class size is −2.5 (bigger classes, lower scores). After adding school_funding to the model, the slope for class size changes to −0.8. Explain why the coefficient changed and what this tells you about school_funding.

school_funding is a confounder: it is associated with both class size (schools with less funding have larger classes) and test scores (less funding means fewer resources and lower scores). In the simple model, class size was getting "blame" for the score drop that was actually due to low funding. After controlling for funding, the remaining effect of class size shrinks from −2.5 to −0.8, meaning much of the original association was driven by the confounding effect of school funding.

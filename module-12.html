Part A Learn: Model Selection Pitfalls
01 The select-then-infer fallacy — fishing for significance

This is the single biggest pitfall in applied statistics: if you look at your data to choose your model, then test that model on the same data, your p-values are invalid.

Flawed workflow

  1. Look at correlations of 20 predictors with Y
  2. Pick the 3 that look strongest
  3. Fit lm(y ~ x5 + x12 + x17)
  4. Report p-values from summary()

P-values are meaningless here!

Valid workflow

  1. Use theory/prior knowledge to choose predictors
  2. Pre-register your model before seeing data
  3. Fit the pre-specified model
  4. Report p-values from summary()

P-values are valid!

Why it fails: When you pick predictors based on the data, you're guaranteed to find ones that look significant — even if they're just noise. The p-value assumes the model was chosen independently of the data, and you violated that assumption.
Analogy: It's like shooting arrows at a wall, then painting a bullseye around wherever they landed. Of course you'll "hit" the target — but it doesn't mean you're a good shot.
02 Multiple testing problem — expected false positives

Even if nothing is real (every predictor is pure noise), testing at $\alpha = 0.05$ means you'll get a false positive 5% of the time.

$$\text{Expected false positives} = \alpha \times \text{number of tests} = 0.05 \times 20 = 1$$

Test 20 predictors? Expect 1 to come out "significant" by chance alone. Test 100? Expect 5.

Key formula for the exam:
Expected false positives = $\alpha \times$ (number of tests)

At $\alpha = 0.05$ with 100 tests: $0.05 \times 100 = 5$ false positives expected.
Think of it this way: If you flip a fair coin 20 times, you wouldn't be surprised to see a few heads in a row. That doesn't mean the coin is biased. Similarly, with 20 predictors, some will look significant by pure chance.

This is why looking at data first, then picking the "best" predictors, inflates your false positive rate way beyond 5%.

03 Adjusted $R^2$ vs $R^2$ — why more predictors can lie

$R^2$ (coefficient of determination) measures the proportion of variance in $Y$ explained by the model. But it has a fatal flaw:

$R^2$ ALWAYS increases (or stays the same) when you add more predictors — even if the new predictor is total garbage. Adding random noise as a predictor will still bump $R^2$ up slightly.

$R^2$

  • Always goes up with more predictors
  • Rewards model complexity
  • Not good for comparing models with different numbers of predictors

Adjusted $R^2$

  • Penalizes for extra predictors
  • Can go down if a useless predictor is added
  • Use this for comparing models
$$R^2_{\text{adj}} = 1 - \frac{(1 - R^2)(n - 1)}{n - p - 1}$$

where $n$ = sample size, $p$ = number of predictors. As $p$ increases, the denominator shrinks, penalizing the score.

Exam rule: When comparing models with different numbers of predictors, always use adjusted $R^2$, not $R^2$. A model with higher $R^2$ but lower adjusted $R^2$ is overfitting.
04 Overfitting — fitting noise instead of signal

Overfitting happens when your model captures the random noise in your training data rather than the true underlying pattern.

Good fit

  • Captures the real trend
  • Predicts well on new data
  • Reasonable number of predictors

Overfit

  • Captures noise and quirks of this specific dataset
  • Predicts poorly on new data
  • Too many predictors relative to sample size
Key insight: More predictors does NOT mean better predictions on new data. A model with 50 predictors and $R^2 = 0.95$ on the training data might do worse on new data than a simpler model with 3 predictors and $R^2 = 0.60$.

Signs of overfitting:

  • High $R^2$ but low adjusted $R^2$
  • Many predictors are not significant
  • Model performs much worse on new/held-out data
05 Pre-registration & theory-driven models — the ethical solution

The solution to all the pitfalls above is straightforward: decide your model BEFORE seeing the data.

Pre-registration means: You write down your hypotheses, your model specification, and your analysis plan before collecting or looking at data. Then you follow that plan exactly.

Theory-driven model selection:

  • Choose predictors based on domain knowledge or prior research
  • Decide on interactions based on theory about how variables relate
  • Do NOT peek at correlations, scatter plots, or p-values to choose what to include
The bright line:
Using data to explore and generate hypotheses = fine (but call it exploratory)
Using data to select a model, then claiming valid p-values = NOT fine

If the model was chosen based on the data, any resulting inference (p-values, confidence intervals) is unreliable.
What about backward selection / stepwise regression? These are data-driven selection methods. Models produced this way should be labeled as exploratory. The p-values from a stepwise-selected model are inflated and should not be trusted for confirmatory inference.
Part B On the Exam: Model Selection Questions
06 Exam Q Type: "Is this a valid analysis?"

These questions describe a researcher's workflow and ask you to evaluate whether their inference is valid.

A researcher has data on 50 variables. They compute correlations between each variable and the outcome, select the 5 with the highest correlations, fit a regression model, and report that 3 of the 5 have significant p-values. Is this a valid analysis?

Loses points

"No, because they should have used more variables."

Misses the actual problem entirely.

Full marks

"No. The researcher selected predictors based on the data, then tested those same predictors on the same data. This is the select-then-infer fallacy. The p-values are invalid because the model was chosen to look good on this data. With 50 variables tested, we would expect some to appear significant by chance alone."
Red flags in a described workflow:
• "looked at correlations first" → select-then-infer
• "tried several models and picked the best" → select-then-infer
• "removed non-significant predictors" → data-driven selection
• "used stepwise regression" → data-driven selection

All of these invalidate subsequent p-values.
Your Turn

A data scientist fits 15 different regression models on the same dataset, picks the one with the highest $R^2$, and reports its p-values as evidence that the predictors matter. What's wrong?

This is the select-then-infer fallacy. By fitting 15 models and choosing the best one, the data scientist used the data to select the model. The resulting p-values are invalid because they don't account for the model selection process. The model was cherry-picked to look good on this data, so it would naturally have small p-values, even if the predictors are noise.
07 Exam Q Type: Expected false positives from multiple testing

Typical exam question:

If you test 100 predictors at $\alpha = 0.05$ and none of them are truly related to the outcome, how many would you expect to be statistically significant by chance alone?

Answer: $0.05 \times 100 = 5$. You'd expect about 5 predictors to appear significant purely by chance.
Expected false positives = alpha x number of tests alpha = 0.05, tests = 100 ==> 0.05 x 100 = 5 alpha = 0.05, tests = 20 ==> 0.05 x 20 = 1 alpha = 0.01, tests = 100 ==> 0.01 x 100 = 1
Your Turn

A genetics study tests 10,000 genes for association with a disease at $\alpha = 0.05$. If none of the genes are truly associated, how many would appear significant?

$0.05 \times 10{,}000 = 500$ genes would appear significant by chance. This is why genomics studies use much stricter significance thresholds (like $\alpha = 5 \times 10^{-8}$).
08 Exam Q Type: $R^2$ vs adjusted $R^2$ — which model is better?

Typical exam question:

Model A has 3 predictors, $R^2 = 0.72$, adjusted $R^2 = 0.70$.
Model B has 10 predictors, $R^2 = 0.78$, adjusted $R^2 = 0.68$.
Which model would you prefer for prediction on new data?

Answer: Model A. Although Model B has a higher $R^2$, its adjusted $R^2$ (0.68) is lower than Model A's (0.70). The higher $R^2$ is likely due to overfitting — Model B has 10 predictors capturing noise. The drop from $R^2$ to adjusted $R^2$ in Model B (0.78 → 0.68) shows that many of its extra predictors are not contributing meaningfully.

Part C Quiz: Model Selection Pitfalls
09 4-Question Quiz
Q1

A researcher examines scatter plots of 30 potential predictors against the outcome, picks the 4 that show the clearest linear pattern, fits a regression, and reports significant p-values. Explain why this analysis is problematic.

This is the select-then-infer fallacy. The researcher used the data to choose which predictors to include (by looking at scatter plots), then performed inference on the same data. The p-values assume the model was specified independently of the data, which was violated. With 30 predictors examined, some will show apparent linear patterns by chance, even if no real relationship exists. The reported p-values are inflated and unreliable.
Q2

You test 40 different predictors at $\alpha = 0.05$ and none of them are truly related to the outcome. How many would you expect to come out as statistically significant? Show your calculation.

Expected false positives $= \alpha \times \text{number of tests} = 0.05 \times 40 = 2$.

You would expect about 2 predictors to appear statistically significant by chance alone.
Q3

A colleague fits two models:
Model 1: 2 predictors, $R^2 = 0.55$, adjusted $R^2 = 0.53$
Model 2: 8 predictors, $R^2 = 0.61$, adjusted $R^2 = 0.51$
They choose Model 2 because "$R^2$ is higher." What would you tell them?

$R^2$ always increases with more predictors, so comparing $R^2$ across models with different numbers of predictors is misleading. Adjusted $R^2$ should be used instead because it penalizes for unnecessary predictors. Model 1 has a higher adjusted $R^2$ (0.53 vs 0.51), suggesting that Model 2's extra 6 predictors are overfitting — capturing noise rather than signal. Model 1 is the better choice.
Q4

Which of the following workflows produces valid p-values? Explain why for each.

(a) A psychologist hypothesizes, based on prior literature, that sleep and exercise predict stress. They collect data and fit lm(stress ~ sleep + exercise).
(b) A marketing analyst runs correlations for 25 metrics, finds 3 correlated with sales, and fits a model with those 3.
(c) A student uses backward selection to remove non-significant predictors, then reports the final model's p-values.

(a) Valid. The model was specified based on prior theory/literature before looking at this dataset. This is theory-driven, pre-specified analysis.

(b) Invalid. Select-then-infer fallacy. The analyst used the data to choose predictors, then tested on the same data. With 25 metrics, some correlations will appear by chance.

(c) Invalid. Backward selection is a data-driven model selection procedure. The final model was chosen because it looked good on this data. P-values from stepwise/backward-selected models are inflated and unreliable for confirmatory inference.

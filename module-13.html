Part A Learn: Transformations
01 Why transform — fixing LINE condition violations

Linear regression assumes four LINE conditions:

LetterConditionWhat it means
LLinearityThe relationship between $X$ and $Y$ is a straight line
IIndependenceObservations are independent of each other
NNormalityResiduals are approximately normally distributed
EEqual varianceSpread of residuals is constant across all $X$ values (homoscedasticity)

When these conditions are violated — especially L (curved relationship) or E (fan-shaped residuals) — transforming variables can fix the problem.

The idea: If $Y$ vs $X$ is curved, maybe $Y$ vs $\log(X)$ is straight. Or maybe $\log(Y)$ vs $X$ is straight. We transform to make the relationship linear so our regression machinery works properly.
How to detect violations: Look at the residual plot (residuals vs fitted values). A good residual plot shows a random scatter with no pattern. Curves, fans, or systematic shapes indicate violations.
02 Log-linear model: lm(log(y) ~ x) — percentage interpretation

When you log-transform the outcome (Y), you get a model where coefficients have a percentage interpretation.

log_linear.R
# Log-linear model
mod_loglin <- lm(log(sal) ~ rent_1br, data = ds_jobs)
$$\log(\hat{y}) = \hat{\beta}_0 + \hat{\beta}_1 x$$

Interpretation: A 1-unit increase in $X$ is associated with a $(e^{\hat{\beta}_1} - 1) \times 100\%$ change in $Y$.

Shortcut when $\hat{\beta}_1$ is small (roughly $|\hat{\beta}_1| < 0.1$):
A 1-unit increase in $X$ is associated with approximately a $\hat{\beta}_1 \times 100\%$ change in $Y$.

Example: if $\hat{\beta}_1 = 0.03$, then a 1-unit increase in $X$ is associated with roughly a 3% increase in $Y$.

Why does the log give a percentage interpretation?

log(y) = beta_0 + beta_1 * x Exponentiate both sides: y = e^(beta_0) * e^(beta_1 * x) Increase x by 1: y_new = e^(beta_0) * e^(beta_1 * (x+1)) = e^(beta_0) * e^(beta_1*x) * e^(beta_1) = y_old * e^(beta_1) So y is MULTIPLIED by e^(beta_1) ==> multiplicative (percentage) change
03 Double-log model: lm(log(y) ~ log(x)) — elasticity interpretation

When you log-transform both $X$ and $Y$, the coefficient becomes an elasticity — percentage change in $Y$ per percentage change in $X$.

double_log.R
# Double-log model
mod_dlog <- lm(log(price) ~ log(mpg), data = hybrid)
$$\log(\hat{y}) = \hat{\beta}_0 + \hat{\beta}_1 \log(x)$$
Interpretation: A 1% increase in $X$ is associated with a $\hat{\beta}_1\%$ change in $Y$.

Example: if $\hat{\beta}_1 = -1.2$, then a 1% increase in MPG is associated with a 1.2% decrease in price.
This is the cleanest interpretation of all three models. Both sides are in percentages, so the coefficient is a direct "percent-for-percent" relationship. Economists call this the elasticity.
04 Linear-log model: lm(y ~ log(x)) — unit per percent

When you log-transform only the predictor ($X$), changes in $X$ are measured in percentages while $Y$ stays in original units.

linear_log.R
# Linear-log model
mod_linlog <- lm(sal ~ log(rent_1br), data = ds_jobs)
$$\hat{y} = \hat{\beta}_0 + \hat{\beta}_1 \log(x)$$
Interpretation: A 1% increase in $X$ is associated with a $\hat{\beta}_1 / 100$ unit change in $Y$.

Example: if $\hat{\beta}_1 = 5000$ and $Y$ is salary, then a 1% increase in rent is associated with a $50 increase in salary.
Why divide by 100? A 1% increase in $X$ means $X$ is multiplied by 1.01, so $\log(X)$ increases by $\log(1.01) \approx 0.01$. The change in $Y$ is $\hat{\beta}_1 \times 0.01 = \hat{\beta}_1 / 100$.
05 How to choose — the master reference table

Pick the transformation that makes the residual plot look random (no curves, no fans). Here's your cheat sheet:

ModelR codeInterpretation of $\hat{\beta}_1$
Linearlm(y ~ x)1-unit increase in $X$ → $\hat{\beta}_1$ unit change in $Y$
Log-linearlm(log(y) ~ x)1-unit increase in $X$ → $(e^{\hat{\beta}_1}-1) \times 100\%$ change in $Y$
Linear-loglm(y ~ log(x))1% increase in $X$ → $\hat{\beta}_1/100$ unit change in $Y$
Double-loglm(log(y) ~ log(x))1% increase in $X$ → $\hat{\beta}_1\%$ change in $Y$
Caution from Lec 14: "Hunting for the right transformation is a type of model selection." If you try all four and pick the best, your inference (p-values) may be unreliable. Ideally, choose based on theory or domain knowledge, not by maximizing $R^2$.
Decision process:
1. Fit the standard linear model first
2. Check residual plot — is there a curve or fan shape?
3. If $Y$ has a right-skewed distribution or multiplicative relationship → try log(y)
4. If $X$ has a right-skewed distribution → try log(x)
5. Compare residual plots before and after — pick whichever looks more random
Part B On the Exam: Transformation Questions
06 Exam Q Type: Interpret the slope of a log-transformed model

Typical exam question:

A model is fit: lm(log(sal) ~ rent_1br, data = ds_jobs). The slope is $\hat{\beta}_1 = 0.00004$. Interpret this coefficient in context.

Loses points

"For every 1-unit increase in rent, log salary increases by 0.00004."

Technically true but misses the whole point of the log transformation. Must convert to percentage.

Full marks

"A $1 increase in one-bedroom rent is associated with approximately a 0.004% increase in predicted salary (since $0.00004 \times 100 = 0.004\%$). Equivalently, a $100 increase in rent is associated with roughly a 0.4% increase in salary."
Template for log-linear interpretation:
"A [1-unit] increase in [X] is associated with approximately a [$\hat{\beta}_1 \times 100$]% [increase/decrease] in [Y]."

For the exact version: "... associated with a $(e^{\hat{\beta}_1} - 1) \times 100\%$ change in [Y]."
Your Turn

A 1 square foot increase in size is associated with approximately a 0.05% increase in predicted house price ($0.0005 \times 100 = 0.05\%$). Equivalently, an additional 100 square feet is associated with roughly a 5% increase in price.
07 Exam Q Type: Why does log give a percentage interpretation?

This is a "show your reasoning" question. Walk through the algebra:

Model: log(y) = beta_0 + beta_1 * x Exponentiate: y = e^(beta_0 + beta_1 * x) y = e^(beta_0) * e^(beta_1 * x) When x increases by 1: y_new = e^(beta_0) * e^(beta_1 * (x + 1)) = e^(beta_0) * e^(beta_1*x) * e^(beta_1) = y_old * e^(beta_1) So: y_new / y_old = e^(beta_1) The RATIO is e^(beta_1), meaning y is MULTIPLIED by e^(beta_1) for each unit increase in x. Percentage change = (e^(beta_1) - 1) * 100%
The key idea: In a linear model, $\hat{\beta}_1$ is an additive effect (add $\hat{\beta}_1$ to $Y$). In a log-linear model, $e^{\hat{\beta}_1}$ is a multiplicative effect (multiply $Y$ by $e^{\hat{\beta}_1}$). That's why we get percentages.

Choosing the right transformation from a residual plot:

Residual plot patterns:
• Curved (U-shape or inverted U) → try transforming $X$ or $Y$
• Fan shape (wider on right) → try log(Y) to stabilize variance
• Random scatter → no transformation needed
08 Exam Q Type: Choose the correct transformation / interpret double-log

Typical question:

A model is fit: lm(log(price) ~ log(mpg), data = hybrid). The slope is $\hat{\beta}_1 = -1.2$. Interpret this coefficient.

Answer: A 1% increase in MPG is associated with a 1.2% decrease in predicted price. (Both variables are log-transformed, so the coefficient is an elasticity — percent for percent.)

Part C Quiz: Transformations
09 4-Question Quiz
Q1

A model is fit: lm(log(income) ~ years_edu, data = census). The slope is $\hat{\beta}_1 = 0.08$. Interpret this in context.

This is a log-linear model. Each additional year of education is associated with approximately an 8% increase in predicted income ($0.08 \times 100 = 8\%$). More precisely, income is multiplied by $e^{0.08} \approx 1.083$, so about an 8.3% increase per year of education.
Q2

A model is fit: lm(log(revenue) ~ log(ad_spend), data = companies). The slope is $\hat{\beta}_1 = 0.45$. Interpret this in context.

This is a double-log model (elasticity). A 1% increase in advertising spending is associated with a 0.45% increase in predicted revenue. Since the elasticity is less than 1, revenue grows more slowly than ad spending — there are diminishing returns to advertising.
Q3

You fit lm(weight ~ height) and the residual plot shows a clear fan shape (residuals spread out more for taller people). What transformation would you try, and why?

A fan shape in the residual plot indicates non-constant variance (violates the E condition in LINE). Try lm(log(weight) ~ height) — log-transforming the response variable often stabilizes the variance because it compresses the scale of larger values. After transforming, check the residual plot again to confirm the fan shape is gone.
Q4

Write one line of R code to fit a double-log regression predicting price from mileage using the data frame cars_df.

lm(log(price) ~ log(mileage), data = cars_df)

Both the response and predictor are wrapped in log(). The coefficient would be interpreted as: "A 1% increase in mileage is associated with a $\hat{\beta}_1\%$ change in price."

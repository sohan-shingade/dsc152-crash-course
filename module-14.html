Part A Learn: Odds Ratios & Relative Risk
01 Risk vs Odds — two different ways to measure how likely something is

These sound similar but they are not the same thing. Getting them confused is a classic exam mistake.

Risk (Probability)

  • Risk = $P(\text{event})$
  • = successes / total
  • Always between 0 and 1
  • Example: 20 out of 100 → risk = 0.20

Odds

  • Odds = $\frac{P(\text{event})}{P(\text{no event})}$
  • = successes / failures
  • Can be anything from 0 to $\infty$
  • Example: 20 out of 100 → odds = 20/80 = 0.25
$$\text{Risk} = \frac{\text{successes}}{\text{total}} \qquad \text{Odds} = \frac{\text{successes}}{\text{failures}} = \frac{p}{1-p}$$
Quick conversion: If risk = 0.2, then odds = $\frac{0.2}{1 - 0.2} = \frac{0.2}{0.8} = 0.25$ (or "1 to 4"). The denominator in risk is total; the denominator in odds is failures.

Another way people say it: "the odds are 1 to 4 against" (1 success for every 4 failures).

02 Relative Risk (RR) — comparing risks between two groups

Relative Risk is the ratio of the risk in the exposed group to the risk in the unexposed group.

$$RR = \frac{\text{risk}_{\text{exposed}}}{\text{risk}_{\text{unexposed}}} = \frac{p_1}{p_2}$$
RR ValueInterpretation
$RR = 1$No difference — same risk in both groups
$RR = 2$Exposed group has twice the risk of the outcome
$RR = 0.5$Exposed group has half the risk — exposure is protective
Example (from Lec 15 — bacon and colon cancer):
Risk among bacon eaters: $13{,}513 / 229{,}238 = 0.0589$
Risk among non-eaters: $12{,}018 / 240{,}373 = 0.0500$
$RR = 0.0589 / 0.0500 \approx 1.18$ — bacon eaters are about 18% more likely to develop colon cancer.

RR is very intuitive: "twice as likely," "half as likely." That's why news headlines love it.

03 Odds Ratio (OR) — comparing odds between two groups

The Odds Ratio is the ratio of odds (not risks) between two groups.

$$OR = \frac{\text{odds}_{\text{exposed}}}{\text{odds}_{\text{unexposed}}} = \frac{p_1 / (1 - p_1)}{p_2 / (1 - p_2)}$$
OR ValueInterpretation
$OR = 1$No difference — same odds in both groups
$OR = 3$The odds of the outcome are 3 times higher in the exposed group
$OR = 0.4$The odds are 60% lower in the exposed group (protective)
Be precise with language. OR compares odds, not risks. Don't say "3 times more likely" when you mean "the odds are 3 times higher." On the exam, this distinction matters.

A useful property from Lec 15: the odds ratio is symmetric. Calculating OR for exposure (cases vs. controls) gives the same number as calculating OR for disease (exposed vs. unexposed).

04 When to use RR vs OR — study design matters

You can't always use both. The study design determines which measure is valid.

Study DesignRR?OR?
Cross-sectionalYesYes
Cohort (prospective)YesYes
Case-control (retrospective)NoYes
Why can't you use RR in case-control studies? In a case-control design, you sample based on the outcome (e.g., pick 100 people with cancer and 100 without). This distorts the prevalence of the disease in your sample, making risk calculations invalid. But the OR still works because of its symmetry property.
Key fact for the exam: When the disease/outcome is rare, $OR \approx RR$. But when the outcome is common, OR and RR can be very different. Lec 15 shows an example where $OR \approx 3.5$ but $RR \approx 1.91$ for a common disease.

OR also shows up naturally in logistic regression (Module 15) — $e^{\beta}$ gives you an odds ratio. That's another big reason OR is used so often.

05 Computing RR and OR from a 2×2 table

Most exam questions give you a 2×2 contingency table. Here's the layout and how to extract everything.

Disease (Yes)Disease (No)Total
Exposedaba + b
Unexposedcdc + d
Risk (exposed) = a / (a + b) Risk (unexposed) = c / (c + d) RR = [a / (a+b)] / [c / (c+d)] Odds (exposed) = a / b Odds (unexposed) = c / d OR = (a/b) / (c/d) = (a * d) / (b * c)
Shortcut for OR: $OR = \frac{a \cdot d}{b \cdot c}$ — the cross-product of the 2×2 table. Multiply the diagonals.

Worked example

Got SickStayed HealthyTotal
Smokers4060100
Non-smokers1090100
Risk (smokers) = 40/100 = 0.40 Risk (non-smokers) = 10/100 = 0.10 RR = 0.40 / 0.10 = 4.0 Odds (smokers) = 40/60 = 0.667 Odds (non-smokers) = 10/90 = 0.111 OR = 0.667 / 0.111 = 6.0 Shortcut: OR = (40 * 90) / (60 * 10) = 3600 / 600 = 6.0 ✓

Notice how OR = 6 but RR = 4. Because the outcome isn't rare (40% in one group), they diverge. If the outcome were rare (say 2% vs 0.5%), they'd be much closer.

R code for tables

odds_ratio.R
# Create a 2x2 table from data
tab <- table(df$exposure, df$outcome)
tab

# Extract cells manually
a <- tab[1, 1]  # exposed + disease
b <- tab[1, 2]  # exposed + no disease
c <- tab[2, 1]  # unexposed + disease
d <- tab[2, 2]  # unexposed + no disease

# Compute
risk_exp   <- a / (a + b)
risk_unexp <- c / (c + d)
RR <- risk_exp / risk_unexp

OR <- (a * d) / (b * c)
Part B On the Exam: Odds Ratios & Relative Risk
06 Exam Q Type: "Compute the odds ratio from this table"

This is a very common question. You'll get a 2×2 table and need to compute OR step by step.

A study examines whether a new drug reduces infection after surgery.

InfectedNot Infected
Drug1585
Placebo3070

Compute the odds ratio for infection, comparing drug to placebo.

Step 1: Identify the cells a = 15 (drug + infected) b = 85 (drug + not infected) c = 30 (placebo + infected) d = 70 (placebo + not infected) Step 2: Compute odds for each group Odds (drug) = 15 / 85 = 0.176 Odds (placebo) = 30 / 70 = 0.429 Step 3: Compute OR OR = 0.176 / 0.429 = 0.41 Or use the shortcut: OR = (15 * 70) / (85 * 30) = 1050 / 2550 = 0.41
Interpretation: The odds of infection for patients receiving the drug are 0.41 times the odds for patients receiving the placebo. In other words, the drug reduces the odds of infection by about 59%.
07 Exam Q Type: "Interpret the odds ratio in context"

Getting the number right is only half the battle. You also need to interpret it in context using proper wording.

Template for interpreting OR:
"The odds of [outcome] are [OR] times higher/lower for [exposed group] compared to [unexposed group]."

Loses points

"OR = 2.5, so the drug group is 2.5 times more likely to get better."

Says "more likely" instead of "odds are 2.5 times higher." Conflates risk and odds.

Full marks

"The odds of recovery for the drug group are 2.5 times the odds of recovery for the placebo group."
Common trap: If 60 out of 100 patients recover, the risk is 0.6, but the odds are $60/40 = 1.5$. Those are very different numbers! Don't mix them up on the exam. Risk uses total in the denominator; odds uses failures.
Your Turn

A study finds OR = 3.2 for developing asthma, comparing children in homes with mold versus homes without mold. Write a one-sentence interpretation.

The odds of developing asthma for children living in homes with mold are 3.2 times the odds for children living in homes without mold.
08 Exam Q Type: "When should you use RR vs OR?"

The exam may ask you to choose between RR and OR, or explain why one is appropriate in a given context.

Decision rules:
1. Case-control study? → Must use OR. RR is invalid because sampling is based on outcome status.
2. Logistic regression output? → The coefficients give log-odds, so $e^{\beta}$ is an OR, not an RR.
3. Prospective / cross-sectional study? → Either works, but RR is more intuitive.
4. Rare disease? → OR $\approx$ RR, so they're interchangeable.
Your Turn

A researcher runs a case-control study and reports "patients with the mutation are 2.4 times as likely to develop the disease (RR = 2.4)." What's wrong with this claim?

In a case-control study, you cannot compute a valid relative risk because subjects are sampled based on their outcome status, distorting the prevalence of the disease. The researcher should report the odds ratio instead. Additionally, saying "2.4 times as likely" describes risk, not odds, which compounds the error.
Part C Quiz: Odds Ratios & Relative Risk
09 4-Question Quiz
Q1

In a clinical trial, 250 patients receive a treatment and 250 receive a placebo. Among treated patients, 50 experience side effects. Among placebo patients, 25 experience side effects. Compute the risk of side effects in each group and the relative risk.

Risk (treatment) = 50 / 250 = 0.20
Risk (placebo) = 25 / 250 = 0.10
RR = 0.20 / 0.10 = 2.0

Patients receiving the treatment have twice the risk of experiencing side effects compared to placebo patients.
Q2

Using the same data from Q1, compute the odds ratio for side effects (treatment vs. placebo).

Side EffectsNo Side Effects
Treatment50200
Placebo25225
Odds (treatment) = 50 / 200 = 0.25
Odds (placebo) = 25 / 225 = 0.111
OR = 0.25 / 0.111 = 2.25

Or using the shortcut: OR = $(50 \times 225) / (200 \times 25) = 11{,}250 / 5{,}000 = 2.25$

Notice that OR (2.25) ≠ RR (2.0). The outcome isn't super rare (20% in treatment group), so they diverge.
Q3

A study reports an odds ratio of 0.65 for hospitalization comparing vaccinated to unvaccinated patients. Write a complete interpretation of this OR in context.

The odds of hospitalization for vaccinated patients are 0.65 times the odds for unvaccinated patients. In other words, vaccination is associated with a 35% reduction in the odds of hospitalization.
Q4

True or false for each statement:

(a) Relative risk can be computed from case-control studies.
(b) When the outcome is rare, OR $\approx$ RR.
(c) If risk = 0.6, then odds = 0.6.
(d) OR = 1 means no association between exposure and outcome.

(a) False — RR is invalid in case-control studies because sampling by outcome distorts disease prevalence.
(b) True — when the outcome is rare, $p$ is small, so $1-p \approx 1$, and odds $\approx$ risk.
(c) False — if risk = 0.6, then odds = $0.6 / 0.4 = 1.5$ (very different!).
(d) True — OR = 1 means the odds are the same in both groups, i.e., no association.

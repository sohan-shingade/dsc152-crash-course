0. WTF Are $\eta$, $\varepsilon$, and Shocks?

If you're lost on the notation, start here. Everything else builds on this.

Start With What You Already Know: Regular Regression

You fit lm(pts ~ opp_def) and get:

$$\text{pts}_i = \hat{\beta}_0 + \hat{\beta}_1 \cdot \text{opp\_def}_i + e_i$$

That $e_i$ is the residual — the gap between what the model predicted and what actually happened. You can compute it: residuals(model) gives you a number for every game.

In regular regression, we assume each $e_i$ is a random, independent draw. Game 5's residual has nothing to do with Game 6's.

That assumption is wrong for time series. If Brunson scored way above his prediction in Game 5, he's probably above it in Game 6 too (momentum, matchups, fatigue). The residuals are correlated.

The Two Layers: $\eta$ and $\varepsilon$

Here's the key. Forget Greek letters for a second. There are two layers of "randomness":

$\varepsilon_t$ — Pure Random Shock

Like rolling a die. Completely unpredictable. Each one is independent of every other.

This is the raw material — the true randomness in the universe.

Example: Brunson's shoelace comes untied in the 3rd quarter. Totally random. Won't happen again tomorrow.

You never observe these directly.

$\eta_t$ — The Actual Error

The real gap between the model's prediction and reality for time $t$.

In regular regression, $\eta_t = \varepsilon_t$ (errors ARE the shocks — independent). In time series, $\eta_t$ is built FROM $\varepsilon$ shocks but is correlated across time.

Example: Brunson's total deviation from predicted points tonight. This IS correlated with yesterday's.

Estimated by residuals from your fitted model.

The whole point of AR/MA: describe how pure random shocks ($\varepsilon$, independent) get turned into correlated errors ($\eta$). That's it. That's the entire idea.

The Two Machines That Turn Shocks Into Correlated Errors

Think of it like a factory. Raw material ($\varepsilon$, pure randomness) goes in. Correlated errors ($\eta$) come out.

The MA Machine

$$\eta_{\text{today}} = \varepsilon_{\text{today}} + 0.7 \times \varepsilon_{\text{yesterday}}$$

Today's error = today's shock + some of yesterday's shock.

Simple. Yesterday's shock bleeds into today. By tomorrow it's gone forever.

Analogy: Brunson has a bad shooting night (random shock). Next game he's still rattled — 70% of that bad night bleeds in. By Game 3 it's gone.

The AR Machine

$$\eta_{\text{today}} = 0.65 \times \eta_{\text{yesterday}} + \varepsilon_{\text{today}}$$

Today's error = fraction of yesterday's error + today's shock.

Key difference: feeds back on itself. Yesterday's error included the day-before's error, which included… The shock echoes.

Analogy: Brunson tweaks his ankle. Today's dip carries into tomorrow, which carries into the next day, gradually fading but never fully gone.

Spot the difference:
MA uses $\varepsilon_{\text{yesterday}}$ (yesterday's shock — one specific random event)
AR uses $\eta_{\text{yesterday}}$ (yesterday's total error — which already contains all past shocks)

That's why AR effects linger forever (it's recursive) and MA effects die after $q$ steps (it only looks back at specific shocks).

What You Actually DO (You Never Touch $\eta$ or $\varepsilon$)

In practice, Arima() handles all the $\eta$/$\varepsilon$ math internally. Your job:

  1. Fit lm() → get residuals (these estimate $\eta$)
  2. Plot ACF/PACF of residuals → see if they're correlated
  3. If correlated → pick AR or MA based on the pattern
  4. Fit Arima() with the right order
  5. Read the output → interpret the $\beta$ coefficients normally

You never compute $\eta$ or $\varepsilon$ by hand. You never even see them. You just tell Arima() which machine to use.

Cheat Sheet: What Every Symbol Means

Symbol What it is Can you see it?
$y_t$ Actual observed value (e.g., points scored in game $t$) Yes — it's your data
$x_t$ Predictor value (e.g., opponent defensive rating) Yes — it's your data
$\hat{\beta}_0, \hat{\beta}_1$ Estimated regression coefficients Yes — in output table
$e_t$ Residual = $y_t - \hat{y}_t$. Estimates $\eta_t$. Yes — residuals(model)
$\eta_t$ True error. Correlated across time. Built from $\varepsilon$'s. No — theoretical
$\varepsilon_t$ Pure random shock. Independent. The raw randomness. No — theoretical
$\phi_1, \phi_2$ AR parameters (how much past errors carry forward) Yes — ar1, ar2 in output
$\theta_1$ MA parameter (how much past shocks bleed in) Yes — ma1 in output
Bottom line: $\eta$ and $\varepsilon$ are theoretical — they explain WHY residuals are correlated. You never compute them. Arima() estimates $\phi$ and $\theta$ (which you DO see in the output), and uses them to fix the standard errors. That's the whole game.

1. Why lm() Breaks on Time Series

The Independence Assumption Under the Hood

Standard linear regression computes standard errors using:

$$\text{SE}(\hat{\beta}_1) = \frac{\hat{\sigma}}{\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i - \bar{x})^2}}$$

This formula assumes every residual $e_i$ is an independent draw from $N(0, \sigma^2)$. It treats $n$ data points as $n$ independent pieces of information.

With time series data, consecutive residuals are correlated: $\text{Cor}(e_t, e_{t-1}) \neq 0$. You have fewer effective observations than $n$, so the formula above gives the wrong answer.

Which Direction Does It Go Wrong?

Depends on the sign of autocorrelation:

Positive autocorrelation

Consecutive residuals tend to be similar (both positive or both negative).

$\text{SE}_{\text{lm}}$ is too small
$\Rightarrow$ p-values too small
$\Rightarrow$ Type I error inflated

Negative autocorrelation

Consecutive residuals tend to alternate sign (bouncing).

$\text{SE}_{\text{lm}}$ is too large
$\Rightarrow$ p-values too large
$\Rightarrow$ power decreases

Exam-ready quote (direct from Lecture 19): "Failing to account for time dependency in your data can lead to incorrect inference. In every example here, we showed a loss of power, but other things could happen."

How Arima() Fixes This

Arima() models the correlation structure explicitly. Instead of assuming $e_t \overset{iid}{\sim} N(0, \sigma^2)$, it says:

$$y_t = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_t + \eta_t \quad \text{where } \eta_t \text{ follows AR}(p) \text{ or MA}(q)$$

By modeling $\eta_t$, the standard errors for $\hat{\beta}_1$ are computed correctly, accounting for how much information the correlated data actually provides.

2. AR vs MA: Full Breakdown

The Two Error Models

AR(p) — Autoregressive

$$\eta_t = \phi_1 \eta_{t-1} + \phi_2 \eta_{t-2} + \cdots + \phi_p \eta_{t-p} + \varepsilon_t$$

Current error depends on its own past values $(\eta)$.

Shock life: Infinite (decaying). Echoes forward because $\eta_{t-1}$ feeds into $\eta_t$, which feeds into $\eta_{t+1}$, etc.

Analogy: Momentum — a hot streak perpetuates itself.

MA(q) — Moving Average

$$\eta_t = \varepsilon_t + \theta_1 \varepsilon_{t-1} + \theta_2 \varepsilon_{t-2} + \cdots + \theta_q \varepsilon_{t-q}$$

Current error depends on past random shocks $(\varepsilon)$.

Shock life: Exactly $q$ steps. After that, the shock is no longer in the formula.

Analogy: Hangover — bad night affects you for $q$ days, then gone.

Critical distinction: AR uses $\eta$ (error feeds back on itself). MA uses $\varepsilon$ (independent shocks). They look similar but behave very differently.

Identification Table: ACF/PACF → Model

Model ACF Pattern PACF Pattern order
$\text{AR}(1)$ Tails off Cuts off after lag 1 c(1, 0, 0)
$\text{AR}(2)$ Tails off Cuts off after lag 2 c(2, 0, 0)
$\text{MA}(1)$ Cuts off after lag 1 Tails off c(0, 0, 1)
$\text{MA}(2)$ Cuts off after lag 2 Tails off c(0, 0, 2)
Mirror pattern: AR and MA swap ACF/PACF roles. AR: PACF cuts off (tells you $p$). MA: ACF cuts off (tells you $q$).

Simulating MA(1) — From Lecture 18 (NBA Example)

True model: $\text{pts}_t = -38 + 0.55 \cdot x_t + \eta_t$ where $\eta_t = \varepsilon_t - 0.55\varepsilon_{t-1}$ and $\varepsilon_t \sim N(0, 8)$.

simulate_ma1.R 
# Generate MA(1) errors for 82 NBA games
set.seed(1)
eps <- rnorm(83, mean = 0, sd = 8)  # n+1 shocks needed for MA(1)

eta <- numeric(82)
for (i in 1:82) {
  eta[i] <- eps[i + 1] + (-0.55) * eps[i]  # MA(1): ε_t + θ₁·ε_{t-1}
}

# Build full regression model: y = β₀ + β₁·x + η
pts <- round(-38 + 0.55 * x + eta, 0)
Why 83 epsilons for 82 observations? MA(1) uses $\varepsilon_t$ and $\varepsilon_{t-1}$. For observation $i=1$, you need $\varepsilon_1$ and $\varepsilon_2$, so you generate $n+1 = 83$ shocks total. No burn-in needed for MA.

Simulating AR(2) — From Lecture 19 (Power Study)

True model: $y_t = 2 + 0.65 \cdot y_{t-1} + 0.25 \cdot y_{t-2} + \varepsilon_t$ where $\varepsilon_t \sim N(0, 1)$.

simulate_ar2.R 
# Generate AR(2) data with 2 burn-in values
set.seed(3)
eps <- rnorm(102, mean = 0, sd = 1)  # n + p extra for burn-in

# Burn-in: first p values aren't from the stationary distribution
y <- numeric(102)
y[1] <- 2 + eps[1]                         # only ε available
y[2] <- 2 + 0.65 * y[1] + eps[2]          # only 1 lag available

# Full AR(2) recursion
for (i in 3:102) {
  y[i] <- 2 + 0.65 * y[i - 1] + 0.25 * y[i - 2] + eps[i]
}

# Discard burn-in
y <- y[3:102]  # keep last 100 observations
Why burn-in? AR is self-referential: $y_3$ depends on $y_2$, which depends on $y_1$. The first $p$ values are "warming up" and don't reflect the true distribution. AR($p$) needs $p$ burn-in values. MA does NOT need burn-in because it only uses independent $\varepsilon$ shocks.
AR vs MA simulation cheat sheet:
AR($p$): generate $n + p$ values of $\varepsilon$, compute $n + p$ values of $y$, discard first $p$
MA($q$): generate $n + q$ values of $\varepsilon$, compute $n$ values of $\eta$ directly, no discard

3. The Complete Arima() Workflow

Step-by-Step: Fitting a Time Series Regression

Full workflow from Lecture 18 — predicting Jalen Brunson's points from opponent defensive rating.

Step 1: Fit with lm() as a baseline

step1_baseline.R 
# Standard regression (ignores time structure)
model_lm <- lm(pts ~ opp_def, data = nba_df)
summary(model_lm)  # gives t-values and p-values

This gives you a p-value assuming independent errors. Might be wrong.

Step 2: Check residuals for autocorrelation

step2_check_residuals.R 
# Plot ACF and PACF of lm residuals
acf(residuals(model_lm))   # does it tail off or cut off?
pacf(residuals(model_lm))  # does it tail off or cut off?

# ACF cuts off after lag 1, PACF tails off → MA(1)

Step 3: Fit with Arima() using the identified structure

step3_arima.R 
library(forecast)
model_arima <- Arima(
  y     = nba_df$pts,
  order = c(0, 0, 1),     # MA(1) — identified from ACF/PACF
  xreg  = nba_df$opp_def  # predictor(s)
)

Step 4: Get p-values with coeftest()

step4_pvalues.R 
library(lmtest)
coeftest(model_arima)  # z-values and p-values for all coefficients

# IMPORTANT: summary(model_arima) does NOT give p-values!
# You MUST use coeftest() from the lmtest package.

Multiple Predictors: Build an xreg Matrix

multiple_predictors.R 
# Single predictor — just pass the vector
model <- Arima(nba_df$pts, order = c(0, 0, 1),
               xreg = nba_df$opp_def)

# Multiple predictors — build a matrix with cbind()
X <- cbind(nba_df$DefRtg, nba_df$MIN, nba_df$TO)
model <- Arima(nba_df$pts, order = c(0, 0, 1), xreg = X)
coeftest(model)
Syntax trap: xreg takes a numeric vector or matrix, NOT a formula. You cannot write xreg = ~ DefRtg + MIN. Use cbind().

How to Get P-values — Every Model Type

Model Fit P-values Test stat
Linear regression lm(y ~ x) summary() $t$-value
Logistic regression glm(y ~ x, family="binomial") summary() $z$-value
Time series regression Arima(y, order, xreg) coeftest() $z$-value
Partial F-test lm() both models anova(null, full) $F$-value

Automatic Order Selection

Don't want to read ACF/PACF yourself? Let R decide:

4. Power Simulation Study (Lec 19)

The Experiment

True model with $\beta_1 = 2$ (a real effect exists):

$$y_t = \underbrace{\beta_0 + \beta_1 x_t}_{\text{regression}} + \underbrace{\phi_1 y_{t-1} + \phi_2 y_{t-2}}_{\text{AR(2) structure}} + \varepsilon_t$$

with $\beta_0 = 0,\; \beta_1 = 2,\; \phi_1 = 0.65,\; \phi_2 = 0.25,\; \varepsilon_t \sim N(0, 3),\; x \sim \text{Unif}(0, 10)$.

Question: $H_0: \beta_1 = 0$. We know $H_0$ is false. How often does each method correctly reject?

Full Simulation Code

power_simulation.R 
# Step 1: Data-generating function
gen_AR2_data <- function(n, phi1, phi2) {
  x   <- runif(n + 2, 0, 10)       # n+2 for burn-in
  eps <- rnorm(n + 2, 0, 3)

  y    <- numeric(n + 2)
  y[1] <- 0 + 2 * x[1] + eps[1]
  y[2] <- 0 + 2 * x[2] + phi1 * y[1] + eps[2]

  for (i in 3:(n + 2)) {
    y[i] <- 0 + 2 * x[i] + phi1 * y[i-1] + phi2 * y[i-2] + eps[i]
  }

  # Discard burn-in
  data.frame(y = y[3:(n + 2)], x = x[3:(n + 2)])
}

# Step 2: Run 1000 simulations
library(forecast)
library(lmtest)

lm_reject    <- 0
arima_reject <- 0

for (sim in 1:1000) {
  dat <- gen_AR2_data(n = 100, phi1 = 0.65, phi2 = 0.25)

  # Method 1: lm (ignoring time structure)
  fit_lm <- lm(y ~ x, data = dat)
  p_lm   <- summary(fit_lm)$coefficients["x", "Pr(>|t|)"]
  if (p_lm < 0.05) lm_reject <- lm_reject + 1

  # Method 2: Arima (correctly modeling AR(2))
  fit_arima <- Arima(dat$y, order = c(2, 0, 0), xreg = dat$x)
  p_arima   <- coeftest(fit_arima)["dat$x", "Pr(>|z|)"]
  if (p_arima < 0.05) arima_reject <- arima_reject + 1
}

# Step 3: Compare power
cat("lm power:   ", lm_reject / 1000, "\n")
cat("Arima power:", arima_reject / 1000, "\n")

Typical Results

$$\text{Power}_{\text{lm}} \approx 0.45 \qquad \text{Power}_{\text{Arima}} \approx 0.78$$
The punchline: With AR(2) errors, lm() has roughly half the power. You miss the real effect ($\beta_1 = 2$) more than half the time. Correctly modeling the time structure nearly doubles your detection rate.

The Naive Alternative: t-test on Differences

Another bad approach: compute consecutive differences $d_t = y_{t+1} - y_t$ and run a t-test.

naive_differences.R 
# Don't do this — even worse than lm() in many cases
diffs <- diff(y)
t.test(diffs)

Differencing destroys information about the trend while not fully removing autocorrelation. Even lower power than lm().

Practice 6
Applied Scenario • Power Loss

A sports analyst models a basketball player's points per game as a function of minutes played across 82 games. Two fits:

  • Model A: lm(pts ~ min) gives $p = 0.062$ for min
  • Model B: Arima(pts, order=c(0,0,1), xreg=min) + coeftest() gives $p = 0.004$ for min, and ma1 = -0.40 with $p < 0.001$
(a) The analyst uses Model A and concludes "minutes played is not a significant predictor." Is she correct? Explain using the concept from Lecture 19.
(b) What does ma1 = -0.40 with $p < 0.001$ tell you about this data?
(c) Interpret ma1 = -0.40 in terms of what happens between consecutive games.
(a) She is wrong. She should use Model B's p-value ($p = 0.004$). The significant ma1 proves the errors are autocorrelated, meaning Model A's standard errors are unreliable. This is exactly the loss of power scenario from Lecture 19: lm() missed a real effect because it had incorrect standard errors from ignoring the time structure.

(b) The data has MA(1) autocorrelation. The significant $p < 0.001$ confirms this isn't just noise — the error structure is real. This validates using Arima() instead of lm().

(c) $\theta_1 = -0.40$ means 40% of the previous game's random shock carries into the current game's error, with reversed sign. If a player scored unexpectedly high last game (positive $\varepsilon_{t-1}$), the MA(1) structure predicts the current game's error will be pushed down by $0.40 \times \varepsilon_{t-1}$ — a "regression to the mean" or bounce-back effect.
